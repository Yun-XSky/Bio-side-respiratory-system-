Tracheal System of an Insect

The respiratory system of insects is the tracheal system.

  • There are small pores in the thorax and abdomen called spiracles. These allow the intake of air.
  • Spiracles have valves that open and close to allow air to go in and out.
  • The spiracles lead into a network of tubes called the trachea. The trachea is supported by chitin rings to prevent it from collapsing.
  • The trachea branches into finer tubes called tracheoles.
  • Tracheoles have thin, moist walls and provide a large surface area. They form a vast network that is in direct contact with muscle cells, allowing direct diffusion of oxygen into the cells and carbon dioxide out of the cells.
  • Some insects have air sacs in their tracheal system to speed up the delivery of respiratory gases during active movements.

Respiratory System of a Fish

The respiratory structure of a fish is the gills. Gills are supported by the gill arch and protected by the operculum.

  • Each gill consists of many thin projections called gill filaments.
  • The filaments contain numerous thin, flat projections called lamellae (singular: lamella) which provide a very large surface area for gas exchange.
  • The lamellae are richly supplied with a network of blood capillaries.
  • Counter-current exchange: The flow of water over the gills and the flow of blood in the blood capillaries are in opposite directions. This mechanism maximizes the concentration gradient for the diffusion of oxygen from the water into the blood across the entire surface of the lamella.

Respiratory System of an Amphibian

Amphibians such as frogs have two respiratory structures: moist skin and lungs.

  • Moist Skin: The skin of a frog is very thin and highly permeable to respiratory gases. It is kept continuously moist by mucus secreted from glands. A dense network of blood capillaries lies just under the skin to rapidly transport oxygen and carbon dioxide.
  • Lungs: Frogs also breathe through a pair of lungs. The inner walls of the lungs are folded to form sacs, which increases the total surface area for the exchange of gases.
  • Air enters through the nostril, passes through the buccal cavity, and goes into the lungs through the glottis. The lungs are heavily supplied with blood capillaries to facilitate diffusion.
